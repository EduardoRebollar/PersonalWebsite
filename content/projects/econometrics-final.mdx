## What I was actually testing

The 2025 Stack Overflow Developer Survey shipped with a question I had been waiting for: how often do you use AI tools in your daily work, and how do you feel about them? I wanted to know whether self-reported AI use predicted compensation once you held the obvious confounders flat — country, years of experience, education, organization size, and developer type.

## Method

Stata 18 throughout. I pulled the public-use survey, dropped respondents with missing or non-numeric compensation, winsorized the top and bottom 1% of `ConvertedCompYearly` to blunt the influence of a handful of half-million-dollar outliers, and collapsed the categorical AI-usage variable into a binary indicator (any use vs. `Never`).

The headline regression was log-compensation on AI use, with a high-income-country indicator and controls for `YearsCodePro`, `EdLevel`, `OrgSize`, and `DevType` (collapsed into a smaller taxonomy I built in the CSV pipeline before Stata ever saw the file). The specification I cared about added an interaction between AI use and years of experience, because the prior was an interaction question — does AI flatten or steepen the seniority curve? — not a level question.

## Key finding

The level effect ran negative: AI users start about 14.8% *behind* at zero experience, and on a one-sided test that coefficient is statistically indistinguishable from zero. The interaction was the result that mattered — and it ran backwards from my hypothesis. Each additional year of experience is worth about 2.0% to a developer who doesn't use AI and about 3.3% to one who does (β₃ = +0.013, t = 7.06). AI *complements* experience rather than substituting for it: the two wage curves cross at about 11.4 years, so the payoff concentrates among **senior** developers. The compounding-on-juniors story the productivity literature tells does not show up in these salaries.

## Limitations I want to be loud about

This is a cross-sectional survey of self-selected respondents, not a treatment effect. The respondents who answered "daily" are the same population that opted into a long survey about their relationship to AI, and that's a thicker selection bias than fixed effects can sweep up. The interaction effect is the kind of finding that would not survive a panel, and I would not run a hiring decision off it.
